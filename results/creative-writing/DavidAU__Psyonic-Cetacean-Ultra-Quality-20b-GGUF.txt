Today, the children came.

I'd been watching for them for days, waiting for the telltale signs of hunger and desperation that would lead them to my doorstep. And finally, there they were, stumbling through the trees, their once-rosy cheeks now pale and hollowed.

I busied myself with making them tea, humming a familiar lullaby under my breath as I stirred the pot. They hesitated, exchanging nervous glances, but eventually, the girl launched into their tale. Their father gone, their mother desperate to remarry, their long and arduous journey to find a better life. Tears welled up in my eyes as she spoke, tears of pity and anger both. How can people be so callous? So heartless? Did they not understand how much pain they caused?

"Oh, my poor dearests," I whispered, reaching out to squeeze the girl's hand. "Your tale breaks my witchy heart." I brushed away a tear that slid down my cheek, savoring the salty sting. "But fear not, my darlings. You've come to the right place. I'll take care of you, I will. You'll never be hungry again, never cold or alone."

Their eyes widened with hope, and it twisted in my gut like a hot poker. I knew what I had to do. What I must do, for their own good. For all our sakes. But how? How could I keep them here, safe and sound, without raising suspicion?

Inspiration struck like a bolt of lightning. "Wait here just a moment, sweethearts," I said, standing suddenly. "I have something special for you." I hobbled across the room, bracing myself against the mantelpiece for dramatic effect, and with a flourish, pulled open the door to my secret chamber.

They exchanged worried glances, but the girl nodded bravely, and I smiled to myself in the darkness. Yes, my darlings. Soon enough, everything will be alright.

I crept back to my grimoire, flipping through the yellowed pages until I found the incantation I sought. Carefully, I inked my quill and began to chant the ancient words, my voice echoing eerily in the stillness. "Vivificare corpus, animamque reviviscere, in corporore vitae mortuo, resurgere."

And with a blinding flash of light, he was there, standing before me in all his terrible glory. My love. My Janus.

"You summoned me, my queen?" he rumbled, his eyes gleaming like polished obsidian. "What boon do you request now?"

I flung myself into his arms, sobbing unashamedly. "Janus, my dearest, my only, I have found them! Their souls are pure and strong, and together, we can finally—"

"Enough!" he growled, shoving me away. "I have endured your weeping and wailing for far too long. Bring me the children, or you shall join their mother in the depths."

I swallowed hard, wiping my tears away. "Yes, my king. Of course. This way, my love."

Trembling, I returned to the main room, where the children still sat, waiting patiently. "My apologies, my dears," I said, forcing a lightness into my voice that felt as false as a cheap trinket. "I seem to have misplaced something rather important. I'll just be but a moment more."

I crept back to my secret chamber, my heart pounding so loudly in my chest I feared they could hear it. Janus lounged on my velvet chaise, his eyes fixed on the children like a hungry wolf eyeing its prey. "Be quick about it," he growled.

With a deep breath, I opened the door again, and this time, I didn't bother to hide my smile. "Ah, there you are!" I crooned, holding out my hand. "Here's that special something I promised you."

The children's eyes widened in horror as they saw him, saw my Janus, his body half-rotted and half-made of clay, his eye sockets empty save for smoldering coals that cast a hellish red glow across his gaunt features. They screamed, and screamed, and screamed.

But I didn't hear them anymore. I was too busy laughing, laughing until my sides ached and tears streamed down my face.

Finally, finally, everything was alright.
