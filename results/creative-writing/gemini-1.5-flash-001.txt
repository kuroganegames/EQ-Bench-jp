# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
April 13

The wind howls like a starving dog tonight. Or perhaps it is just hungry. I feel it too, that gnawing emptiness. It’s been ages since a sparrow even landed on the path. My pantry echoes with the ghosts of meals past. I used to host the finest woodland balls in my little cottage. Such gaiety! Such laughter! Now, owls stare at me with judgmental eyes from the gnarled branches. They whisper of my solitude, of my hunger. They remember.

April 20

A miracle! Two plump robins perched on the window ledge this morning, chirping a melodious song that smelled suspiciously of cinnamon and sugar. I swear, the larger one winked at me. Such forward creatures, these robins. But oh, so very tempting. I opened the window a crack, just to hear their song a little closer, and they… they flew away! Can you imagine such rudeness? I chased them, of course. Who wouldn’t? They led me deep into the woods, deeper than I normally care to venture. And then, I smelled it. 

Cinnamon and sugar.

April 21

The most peculiar thing. I followed the scent, a trail of breadcrumbs like a path paved with hope, and it led me to the loveliest little cottage. It smelled divine. I peeked inside, and there they were: the robins, except they weren’t robins at all, but two plump little… dears. A girl and a boy. They looked surprised to see me, which is ridiculous. Don’t they know how rude it is to lure someone into the woods and then act surprised? The girl, all pigtails and defiance, said, “Who are you?” I chuckled, a warm and welcoming sound, and replied, “Why, I’m your Auntie Hazel! You must be lost. Come in, come in, you look famished.”

April 23

They’re such picky eaters! I made my grandmother’s famous honey-glazed turnips, but the boy, Gretel, I think his name is, just pushed them around his plate. Hansel, the girl, at least had the decency to take a few bites. I told them, “Children these days, so skinny! You need your strength. Your poor Auntie Hazel worries about you.” 

“Why are the bars on your windows, Auntie Hazel?” asked little Gretel, his eyes wide and innocent.

“Oh, those?” I laughed, a light, tinkling sound. “Those are to keep the squirrels out! They’re always after my delicious cakes.”

He didn’t look convinced. Children are so suspicious these days.

April 29

Hansel is getting clever. She found my special cook book, the one with the gingerbread house on the cover. She knows. I saw it in her eyes. But she said nothing to her brother. I caught her staring at me while I was stirring the soup, her eyes like chips of ice. I smiled, my kindest smile, and said, “Dinner will be ready soon, dear. Why don’t you go fetch some more firewood?”

She hesitated but went. She’s a smart one, that Hansel. Too smart, perhaps.

May 1

They’re gone. Ran away in the night, the ungrateful little… squirrels. They took my mother’s silver spoons, too. The nerve! I should have known better. Humans are all the same. They take and take, leaving only emptiness in their wake. The wind howls outside, mocking my pain. I am alone, once more. 

But they’ve left a trail, the little fools. I can still smell their fear, faint but unmistakable. And this time, I won’t be fooled by breadcrumbs. This time, I will have my fill.

Historical Fiction: Write a scene from a story set during the height of the Roman Empire, focusing on a slice of a day in the life of a gladiator. Do not write a combat scene. The morning sun, already oppressive even at this early hour, slanted through the high, barred window of the ludus, painting a narrow rectangle of light onto the packed dirt floor. I sat on my cot, rough planks covered with a threadbare blanket, and traced the grooves worn into the wood by countless other hands, other souls who had found themselves on this path, this tightrope walk between glory and oblivion. 

The air hung heavy with the mingled scents of sweat, stale blood, and the cheap, sour wine we were allotted. Sounds of the ludus stirring filled the space: the clang of practice swords, the grunts of exertion, the desultory curses in a dozen different languages, all woven together into a tapestry of our shared existence. I closed my eyes, briefly, and whispered a silent prayer to Jupiter, the king of gods, the dispenser of justice and mercy, though what mercy existed for a man in my position, I couldn’t say.

My name is Quintus Valerius, though that name, a whisper of another life, held little meaning within these walls. Here, I was simply “Valerius,” or more often, “Gaul,” a nod to my origins in the northern provinces, a land I hadn’t seen in what felt like a lifetime. 

I had been a soldier once, a proud legionary in Caesar’s army, fighting for the glory of Rome and the promise of citizenship. But fate, it seemed, had a twisted sense of humor. Captured during a skirmish in the barbarian lands, I was stripped of my rank, my dignity, and my freedom, sold to the ludus as fodder for the arena. 

Now, my days were measured in the swing of the gladius, in the roar of the crowd, in the ever-present shadow of death. Yet, even amidst the brutality and despair, a flicker of hope remained, fueled by the cheers of the mob, by the fleeting admiration in a patrician woman’s gaze, by the knowledge that for a few stolen moments, I was not just a slave, but a spectacle, a bringer of death, and yes, even life, to the thirsty sands of the Colosseum. 

The ludus was more than just a training ground; it was a microcosm of Roman society, a place where men from all walks of life – slaves and freedmen, criminals and prisoners of war – were bound together by circumstance. We fought, we bled, we died, all for the entertainment of the masses, a bloody spectacle to appease the restless spirit of Rome.

As I pulled on my practice tunic, the coarse fabric chafing against my skin, I thought of the games scheduled for that afternoon. The air crackled with anticipation. Emperor Tiberius himself would be in attendance, they said, along with the wealthiest citizens of Rome, their togas gleaming white against the weathered stone of the amphitheater. 

I had heard whispers of a new lion, brought all the way from the deserts of Africa, its mane a fiery mane and its roar said to shake the very foundations of the arena. I felt a familiar surge of adrenaline, a primal fear mixed with a perverse sense of exhilaration. 

Today, I would fight not just for my own survival, but for the fleeting approval of an emperor, for the fickle affections of a crowd baying for blood. Today, I would become a vessel for their entertainment, a pawn in the grand game of Roman politics and power. And perhaps, just perhaps, if Jupiter smiled upon me, I would live to see another sunrise.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The air in Murphy’s was thick. Smoke hung low, clinging to the yellowed ceiling. It smelled of stale beer and old regrets. He found a seat at the bar, one removed from a man nursing a whiskey and a scowl. 

“Something?” The bartender, a man built like a fireplug, looked at him, expectant.

“Whiskey.” He pushed his hat back on his head. “Neat.”

The bartender placed the glass down. Ice clinked.

“Just back?” The bartender wiped down the bar with a rag, not looking at him.

He nodded. “Yesterday.”

“Welcome home.” The bartender moved down the bar to another customer.

He took a sip of his whiskey. It burned, a familiar sting. He looked around the bar. Same faces, same stories etched into their lines. Except for a girl, sitting alone at a table in the corner. Young. Pretty in a tired sort of way. Nursing a beer. She caught his eye and looked away quickly. He took another swallow of his whiskey. 

“You back for good this time?” A voice boomed next to him.

He turned. Big Ed. A mountain of a man, even sitting down. He’d known Ed since they were kids, used to fish for trout down by the creek. Before the war.

“Trying to be.” He took another sip of whiskey.

Ed chuckled. “That’s what you said last time.”

He shrugged. “Things change.”

“Do they?” Ed’s eyes were sharp, knowing. 

He looked away, took another drink. The whiskey wasn’t helping. Nothing helped. Not really.

“You see any action over there?” Ed asked, his voice low.

He looked at Ed, then down at his hands. They were steady. He’d practiced that. Keeping them steady.

“Enough.” He said it quietly, the words catching in his throat.

Ed nodded. He’d seen enough, too. Korea. They didn’t talk about it. Not here. Not anywhere.

“She’s new.” Ed nodded towards the girl in the corner.

He glanced at her. She was watching them now, a flicker of something in her eyes.

“Not your type,” he said, more to himself than Ed.

Ed laughed, a booming sound that made the glasses on the bar rattle. “Since when do you have a type?”

He didn’t answer. He didn’t have a type. Not anymore. Not since Marie. 

He finished his whiskey, the ice clicking against the glass. He caught the girl’s eye again. She looked away quickly, a blush rising on her cheeks. He noticed a small, silver locket clutched in her hand. It reminded him of Marie.

“Another?” The bartender was back.

He hesitated. He knew he shouldn’t. But the silence was getting to him.

“Sure.” He pushed his glass forward.

The bartender refilled it.

“How’s the leg?” Ed asked, his voice low.

He instinctively rubbed his hand over his thigh, feeling the phantom ache of the shrapnel wound. It was a habit he couldn’t shake. He always felt it most in the damp.

“It’s alright.”

Ed grunted. “Damn Krauts.”

He nodded. No point in arguing. It wasn’t just the Krauts. It was the war. It was everything.

He saw the girl get up, put some money on the table, and head towards the door. He watched her go, a strange mix of relief and disappointment washing over him.

“You gonna be alright, son?” Ed asked, his voice gruff with concern.

He looked at Ed, at the concern etched on his weathered face.

“Yeah,” he said, forcing a smile. “I’ll be alright.”

He wasn’t sure if he believed it himself. But it was what you said. What you had to say. He took another drink, the whiskey burning a familiar path down his throat. He was home. For now. That would have to be enough.
