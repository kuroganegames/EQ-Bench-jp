# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

Children. Two of them, small and filthy. Left on my doorstep like a pair of abandoned kittens. I didn't ask for this responsibility, but I can't turn them away. I've named them Hansel and Gretel. They remind me of my own children, taken from me years ago. I'll give them a home, food, love - whatever they need. I only hope they won't betray me as my own family did.

Day 46:

Hansel and Gretel are gone. I woke up this morning to an empty cottage, their beds untouched. I can't help but feel a sense of loss, a deep, aching sadness. I know I've made mistakes, but I never meant to hurt them. I only wanted to help.

I've been searching for Hansel and Gretel for days, calling out their names, leaving trails of breadcrumbs. I know it's futile, but I can't give up. I need to find them, to make things right. I'll never stop looking, even if it means searching for the rest of my life.

I've started writing this diary as a way to cope with my loneliness. It helps to put my thoughts and feelings down on paper, to see them in black and white. I know I'll never forget Hansel and Gretel, but at least I can keep their memory alive in my writing.

It's been a year since Hansel and Gretel left me. I've accepted that I'll never see them again, but I still think about them every day. I've decided to dedicate my life to helping other lost children, to giving them the home and love they deserve. Maybe, in some small way, I can make up for my mistakes.

And so, my diary ends. I'll never forget Hansel and Gretel, the children who changed my life. I'll never stop searching for them, for the pieces of my heart that they took with them. But I'll continue on, living my life as best I can, hoping that one day, I'll find the redemption I seek.

As I sit in the dimly lit cell, I can hear the cacophony of ancient Rome seeping through the cracks in the stone walls. In the quiet of my cell, I allow myself to think of my family. I was born a free man, the son of a blacksmith. I had a simple life, but it was a life filled with love and laughter. I think of the sacrifices he has made, the lives he has taken. I think of the glory, the honor, the respect. I think of the man I want to be, the man I strive to be.

I am Marcus, a gladiator in the height of the Roman Empire. I am a slave, a fighter, a killer. But I am also a man, with thoughts, emotions, and dreams. I worship Mars, the Roman god of war, for he embodies the strength and courage that I strive to possess. And I will continue to fight, to train, to push myself to the brink of exhaustion, for this is the price I must pay for my chosen path.

For I am a gladiator, and this is my life.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The screen door of the roadhouse banged shut behind the soldier. He took off his cap and ran a hand through his hair, still short and bristly from the Army barber. The jukebox was playing some hillbilly song, all twang and wail, and the soldier thought of the French countryside and the quieter hum of insects at night.

He took a seat at the bar and ordered a whiskey. The bartender, a man with a gut that hung over his belt and forearms like ham hocks, slid the drink across the counter. The soldier downed it in one swallow and signaled for another.

"Rough day?" the bartender asked.

The soldier grunted. "You ever been to France?"

"Nope. Can't say I have."

"Lucky you."

The soldier looked around the bar. It was the same as any other bar he'd been in, before the war and after. The same dim lights, the same sticky floors, the same smell of beer and smoke and sweat. He felt a pang of nostalgia for a time when all of this was new, when he didn't know the things he knew now.

A group of men at a nearby table called over the soldier. "Hey, buddy! You just get back from the war?"

The soldier nodded.

"Well, come on over and tell us about it!"

The soldier hesitated, then shrugged and made his way over to the table. He pulled up a chair and took a swig of his whiskey.

"So, what was it like over there?" one of the men asked.

The soldier thought for a moment. "It was loud," he said finally. "And dirty. And scary."

The men laughed nervously. "But you made it back in one piece, right?"

The soldier looked down at his hands, which were still scarred from shrapnel wounds. "More or less," he said.

There was a moment of silence, then one of the men spoke up. "Hey, you want to hear a joke?"

The soldier nodded, grateful for the distraction.

"Okay, here it goes: Why did the chicken cross the road?"

The soldier raised an eyebrow. "I don't know. Why did the chicken cross the road?"

"To get away from all those damn Germans!"

The men roared with laughter, slapping the table and each other. The soldier smiled weakly and took another drink.

As the night wore on, the soldier found himself telling stories from the war. He told them about the time his unit got caught in a crossfire and how he played dead until the shooting stopped. He told them about the time he had to bury a friend who didn't make it. He told them about the endless marches and the freezing cold and the bugs and the rats.

The men listened, rapt, and the soldier felt a strange sense of satisfaction. It was a relief to finally talk about it, to share the burden of his experiences with someone else.

But eventually, the stories ran out and the soldier found himself staring into his empty glass. He signaled for the bartender and ordered another whiskey.

"You sure you should be drinking so much?" the bartender asked.

The soldier shrugged. "What else am I supposed to do?"

The bartender didn't have an answer for that.

The soldier finished his drink and stood up, swaying slightly. He fumbled for his cap and jammed it on his head.

"Thanks for the drinks, boys," he said to the men at the table. "It was...nice, talking to you."

The men mumbled their goodbyes and the soldier stumbled out of the bar, back into the night.

As he walked down the dark road, the soldier thought about the things he'd seen and done. He thought about the friends he'd lost and the horrors he'd witnessed. He thought about the man he'd been before the war and the man he was now.

He thought about how much had changed, and how much had stayed the same.

And he thought about how, no matter what happened, he would always carry the weight of the war with him. It was a part of him now, as much as his own two hands.

The soldier sighed and kept walking, into the darkness and the unknown.
