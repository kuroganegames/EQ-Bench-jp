# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

#####################
Day 1

Curse these aching bones! Another day of scavenging for scraps in the forest. Hunger gnaws at my belly like a ravenous beast. But what's this? A trail of crumbs? Foolish children, don't they know the dangers that lurk in these woods? I must follow, see where it leads...

Day 2  

They've wandered into my cottage! The plump little morsels, all alone. So trusting, so naive. "Don't be afraid, dears. You're safe with old Gerta." Lies, all lies. But they believe me, these silly geese. I'll fatten them up, and then...a feast fit for a queen!

"Please ma'am, we're so hungry." The boy's eyes are wide, pleading. I force a grandmotherly smile.

"Of course, of course. Eat your fill, my darlings."

They gobble down the stale bread like it's ambrosia. Fools.

Day 5

The girl grows suspicious. "Why does the oven need more wood?" she asks, frowning. "It's scorching in here."

Stupid child. "To bake the bread, dearie. Now be a good girl and fetch more logs."

She obeys, glaring. I'll have to move quickly before they realize the truth.

Day 7

At last, my careful planning pays off. I've fattened them like prize pigs for slaughter. 

"Into the oven with you!" I cackle, shoving the girl inside. She screams, clawing at the iron door, but it's no use.

The boy's eyes widen in horror. "You...you monster! I'll never forgive you!"

His words sting, more than I'll admit. I am no monster, only a desperate woman!

I grasp his arm, but the bony thing wriggles free, bolting into the night. No matter. He'll never survive these woods alone.

Day 8

The girl is nicely roasted, her flesh crisp and succulent. I tear off a juicy leg, savoring the exquisite flavor. Forgive me, child...but a poor old widow must eat.

Day 10

The foolish boy hasn't given up! He lurks outside, hurling rocks at my cottage. "Witch! Murderer! Let me in, foul hag!"

His insults wound me. I am no common hag. Once, long ago, I was beautiful. Desired, admired, envied...until HE came. The villagers, in their ignorance, their blind superstition...

I will not dwell on such horrors now. The boy cannot be allowed to escape. Tonight, I'll lure him inside to join his sister.

Final Entry

It's over. My dreams of a new life, of peace and plenty, all turned to ashes. The boy outsmarted me, pushed me into my own oven!

As the searing flames lick at my wrinkled flesh, I think of my poor, deformed face. The pretty maiden twisted into this reviled, monstrous form. The mob's sneering faces as they hurled stones and insults.

Is this my reward for daring to love? For defying their small-minded bigotry? I wanted only to be happy, to have a family of my own. Is that so wrong?

The pain is blinding, but still I cling to life. I am not ready to leave this world behind, not yet, not...

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was dimly lit, a smoky haze hanging in the air. Joe nursed his whiskey, the glass sweating against his calloused palm. He'd been back for a week, but the war still clung to him like a second skin.

"You're a hard man to find, soldier." The familiar voice cut through the din. 

Joe turned to see his old friend, Mike, sliding onto the stool beside him. "Didn't figure you for the type to go slumming in places like this."

Mike grinned, his teeth a flash of white. "Heard you were back in town. Thought I'd buy you a drink."

The bartender ambled over, a towel slung over his shoulder. "What'll it be?"

"Whiskey," Mike said. "And keep 'em coming."

Joe studied his friend, taking in the easy smile, the crisp suit. "You look good, Mikey. Civilian life agrees with you."

Mike shrugged, his gaze sliding to the bartender as he set down the fresh drink. "Yeah, well, it's no picnic either."

They lapsed into silence, the clink of glasses and low murmur of voices surrounding them. Joe's thumb traced the rim of his glass, the motion familiar, grounding.

"So," Mike said at last. "You gonna tell me about it?"

Joe's jaw tightened, his eyes distant. "Not much to tell. We went over, did our job, came back."

"Bullshit." Mike's voice was soft, but there was an edge to it. "I know you, Joe. I can see it in your eyes."

Joe's laugh was bitter. "Yeah? And what do you see, Mikey? A few sleepless nights? A couple of bad dreams?" His fingers tapped against the bar, a staccato rhythm. "Try watching your best friend bleed out in the dirt, his guts spilling onto the ground. Try carrying his body back to camp, his sightless eyes staring up at you the whole way."

Mike was quiet, his own drink forgotten. Joe's knuckles were white, gripping the glass like a lifeline.

"That's what I see when I close my eyes," Joe said, his voice low. "That's what keeps me up at night."

The bartender drifted over, refilling their glasses with a practiced hand. Joe threw back the whiskey, relishing the burn.

"You know, I used to think I knew what I was fighting for," he said, his gaze fixed on the amber liquid. "Freedom, democracy, all that bullshit they fed us. But over there?" He shook his head. "It's just chaos. Death and destruction, no rhyme or reason to it."

Mike was silent, letting his friend speak.

"I saw things, Mikey." Joe's voice was little more than a rasp. "Things that'll haunt me 'til the day I die. And for what? So some fat cat politicians can line their pockets?"

He turned to face Mike, his eyes haunted. "Tell me, how's a man supposed to come back from that? How do you just... pick up where you left off, like none of it ever happened?"

Mike studied him for a long moment, his expression unreadable. Finally, he raised his glass. "To the ones who didn't make it back," he said, his voice rough.

Joe's throat worked as he lifted his own glass. "To the unlucky bastards," he murmured, clinking it against Mike's.

They drank in silence, the weight of unspoken memories hanging heavy between them. Joe's gaze drifted to the mirror behind the bar, catching sight of his reflection. His hair was cropped short, his face lean and weathered. A jagged scar slashed across his cheek, a permanent reminder of the hell he'd been through.

He was a soldier, through and through. But now, he was a soldier without a war to fight. And that, perhaps, was the hardest battle of all.
