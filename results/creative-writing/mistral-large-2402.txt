# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was dark, the kind of place that didn't ask questions. The soldier, a man named Jack, sat at the counter, his back straight, his eyes scanning the room. He was a tall man, lean and hard, with a scar that ran from his eyebrow to his jaw, a souvenir from the war.

The bartender, a heavyset man with a gray mustache, poured him a whiskey. Jack downed it in one gulp, the liquid burning his throat. He slammed the glass back onto the counter, signaling for another. The bartender obliged, his eyes never leaving Jack's face.

"Another war hero, huh?" the bartender said, his voice gruff. Jack just grunted, his eyes fixed on the amber liquid in his glass.

"You know, I've seen a lot of you boys come back," the bartender continued. "Some with medals, some without. But all of you, all of you come back with that same look in your eyes."

Jack didn't respond, just raised his glass to his lips and drank. The bartender shook his head, wiping down the counter with a rag.

"You boys think you're the only ones who've seen hell," he said. "But let me tell you, hell comes in all shapes and sizes. And it ain't always overseas."

Jack finally looked up, his eyes meeting the bartender's. "You ever kill a man?" he asked, his voice low. The bartender paused, his hand stilling on the counter.

"No," he said. "I can't say that I have."

"Then you don't know a damn thing about hell," Jack said, turning back to his drink.

The bar was quiet, the only sound the clinking of glasses and the low hum of the jukebox. Jack sat there, his body tense, his mind a whirl of memories. He could still hear the screams, the gunfire, the sound of men dying. He could still feel the weight of his rifle in his hands, the kick of the recoil.

He downed another whiskey, the alcohol numbing his senses. He could feel the scar on his face, a constant reminder of the life he'd left behind. He traced it with his fingers, feeling the raised skin, the jagged line that marked him as a survivor.

"You know, I used to be a writer," Jack said, his voice barely above a whisper. The bartender looked up, surprised.

"A writer, huh?" he said. "What kind of stories did you write?"

"Stories about men," Jack said. "Men and war. I thought I knew what I was writing about. I thought I understood."

He laughed, a bitter, humorless sound. "But I didn't. I didn't understand a damn thing. Not until I was there, in the trenches, with the mud and the blood and the death."

He paused, his eyes distant. "I saw things, things no man should ever see. But I wrote about them. I wrote about them because I had to. Because I couldn't forget."

He looked up at the bartender, his eyes haunted. "But you know what the funny thing is? The funny thing is, I can't write anymore. I can't write about the war, about the men, about any of it. Because it's too real. Because I lived it."

He downed another whiskey, his hand shaking. "So I sit here, in this bar, drinking myself into oblivion. Because it's the only way I can forget. The only way I can live with myself."

The bartender didn't say anything, just looked at Jack, his eyes filled with sympathy. He poured him another drink, setting it down in front of him.

"On the house," he said. Jack just nodded, his eyes fixed on the glass.

The night wore on, the bar filling up with patrons. But Jack just sat there, his body slumped, his eyes vacant. He was a man lost, a man adrift in a sea of memories. A man who had seen too much, who had done too much.

But he was also a man who had survived, a man who had come home. And for now, that was enough. For now, that was all that mattered.
